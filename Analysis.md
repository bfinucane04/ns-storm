## Milestone 3: Systems Analysis
### Resilient by Design: Storm Infrastructure vs. Emergency Response

---

## 1. System Archetype Identification

### Primary Archetype: Growth and Underinvestment

The **Growth and Underinvestment** archetype describes a system in which demand grows toward a natural limit, that limit could be raised through investment, but investment is delayed or withheld — often because the system appears to be "managing" in the short term. The result is that performance erodes gradually until a crisis makes the underinvestment visible, at which point reactive spending occurs — too late, and at far greater cost than proactive investment would have required.

This structure maps precisely onto the global disaster resilience system.

**Generic archetype structure:**
- Growing demand → approaches a performance limit
- Limit could be raised by investment
- Investment is delayed due to perceived adequacy of current performance or budget pressure
- Performance erodes → crisis → reactive investment → temporary relief → cycle repeats

**Mapping to the disaster resilience system:**

| Generic Element | Project-Specific Variable |
|---|---|
| Growing demand | Disaster frequency and intensity (R1 reinforcing loop: Climate Change Intensity → disasters) |
| Performance limit | Infrastructure Quality and Emergency Response Capacity ceilings |
| Investment needed to raise limit | Sustained federal hazard mitigation spending |
| Delay mechanism | Budget Constraint (B1 loop): recovery costs consume budgets that could fund prevention |
| Performance erosion | Rising economic losses, longer recovery times, growing protection gap |
| Crisis trigger | Major events (Katrina, Sandy, Harvey/Irma/Maria) that force reactive spending spikes |

**Evidence from the data:**

Figure 1 (FEMA Disaster Declarations, 1953–2024) shows the "growth" pressure directly: the 5-year rolling average of disaster declarations rises from roughly 200 per year in the 1960s to over 1,500 by the 2010s, with the upward trajectory accelerating post-2010 even when COVID-19 biological declarations are excluded. This is the demand side of the archetype operating visibly over seven decades.

Figure 3 (FEMA Declarations vs. Hazard Mitigation Spending, 1989–2024) reveals the "underinvestment" half with equal clarity. Federal mitigation spending does not track disaster growth — it spikes reactively after catastrophic events (Katrina: ~$1.6B spike; Harvey/Irma/Maria: ~$3B spike) then falls back. Between 1995 and 2015, disaster declarations more than doubled while mitigation spending stagnated below $1 billion per year. This reactive, event-driven pattern is the hallmark of Growth and Underinvestment: investment follows collapse rather than preventing it.

The global economic data (Figure 2) confirms the consequence: average annual disaster losses grew from approximately $70 billion in the 1980s to $180 billion by the 2000s, with the 5-year rolling average now trending above $150 billion annually. The Maintenance Reality Check loop (B2) further suppresses investment: as the municipal budget is drained by recovery costs, infrastructure maintenance is deferred, which accelerates the degradation of the very infrastructure that could have reduced disaster severity.

### Secondary Archetype: Shifting the Burden

The **Shifting the Burden** archetype describes a situation where a symptomatic fix — one that relieves immediate pressure — is repeatedly applied instead of the fundamental solution, to the point that the fundamental solution capacity atrophies.

**Mapping to the disaster resilience system:**

| Generic Element | Project-Specific Variable |
|---|---|
| Problem symptom | Disaster damage and human suffering after each event |
| Symptomatic fix | Emergency response spending (immediate relief, recovery funds) |
| Fundamental solution | Pre-disaster infrastructure investment and risk-informed planning |
| Side effect | Emergency response spending crowds out mitigation budgets; political urgency rewards response over prevention |
| Atrophied capacity | Long-term infrastructure resilience; community adaptive capacity |

The critical dynamic is the political economy of disaster spending. Emergency response is visible, morally urgent, and electorally rewarded. Prevention is invisible — its successes are disasters that didn't happen. This creates a structural bias toward symptomatic response. As Figure 3 shows, the federal government has repeatedly increased mitigation spending in the immediate aftermath of catastrophic events, only to allow it to fall back once the political pressure dissipates. The system keeps returning to the symptomatic fix (response) rather than committing to the fundamental solution (prevention), and with each cycle, the infrastructure deficit grows larger and the burden on emergency response grows heavier.

The R2 loop in the CLD captures this directly: greater Emergency Response Capacity reduces perceived disaster severity, which reduces political pressure to fund prevention, which leaves infrastructure vulnerable to the next event.

---

### Key Variables and Feedback Loops

The CLD identifies four loops driving system behavior:

- **R1 (Infrastructure Investment Spiral):** Investment → Infrastructure Quality → reduced disaster impact → political will for further investment. This virtuous cycle is currently operating too weakly — investment is insufficient to sustain it.
- **R2 (Emergency Response Capability Spiral):** Emergency Response Capacity → faster recovery → reduced recovery costs → greater funding availability → more capacity. This loop is partially active but is undermined by the B1 constraint.
- **B1 (Budget Constraint):** Rising Recovery Costs → reduced Municipal Budget → less Investment. This is the core constraint throttling both reinforcing loops.
- **B2 (Maintenance Reality Check):** Reduced Municipal Budget → deferred Maintenance → degraded Infrastructure Quality → greater disaster severity → higher recovery costs. This is the slow-moving but devastating consequence of chronic underinvestment.

## 2. Scenario Narratives

### Scenario 1: Status Quo — The Sendai Framework Expires Without Structural Change

*2026–2036*

The Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction reaches its 2030 deadline without a successor agreement that meaningfully changes the structural dynamics of disaster financing. National governments continue their reactive spending patterns: mitigation budgets fluctuate between $0.5 billion and $3 billion annually based on which disasters captured public attention in the prior year, while emergency response and recovery spending remains the dominant claim on federal budgets after each major event.

By 2030, global disaster losses — already trending above $150 billion per year in the 5-year rolling average — approach $200 billion annually as climate-driven intensification continues and as more assets are exposed in coastal and fire-prone zones. The B1 loop tightens progressively: each major hurricane season or wildfire year draws down municipal and federal budgets, leaving less available for the mitigation projects that could reduce the next event's costs. Infrastructure quality stagnates or degrades in lower-income jurisdictions that cannot access federal grants, while wealthier municipalities slowly upgrade their own systems.

The equity dimension worsens measurably. Small Island Developing States — already positioned in the upper-right quadrant of Figure 4 with high exposure and near-zero coping capacity — face compounding disaster cycles with no recovery interval. The $263 billion global insurance protection gap widens further. By 2036, the system is spending more on disasters than in any prior decade while producing worse outcomes for the most vulnerable populations. The Shifting the Burden dynamic has fully asserted itself: emergency response capacity has grown relative to infrastructure quality, but the underlying risk has grown faster than either.

*Key uncertainty:* Whether a catastrophic event (a direct hit on a major urban center, or a sequence of overlapping disasters) triggers the kind of political rupture that forces structural change, as Katrina did in 2005 — but at far greater scale.

---

### Scenario 2: Intervention A — Infrastructure-First Investment

*2026–2036*

Beginning in 2027, a coalition of high-income nations — drawing on the demonstrated 6:1 return on mitigation investment — commits to doubling pre-disaster hazard mitigation spending as a fixed share of GDP rather than a reactive post-disaster allocation. In the United States, federal hazard mitigation grants increase from approximately $2–3 billion annually to $6–8 billion, structured as multi-year commitments that allow state and local planners to design and execute large-scale infrastructure projects rather than rushing to spend post-disaster supplementals.

The R1 reinforcing loop begins to strengthen over a 5–7 year horizon. Upgraded stormwater infrastructure, hardened coastal barriers, and resilient building codes reduce disaster severity per event even as event frequency continues to rise. By 2033, early modeling suggests that the 5-year rolling average of economic losses is growing more slowly than the previous trend — not declining absolutely, but bending the curve. The B1 constraint loosens modestly: fewer catastrophic losses mean more budget available for the next investment cycle.

However, the distributional effects are uneven. Infrastructure investment naturally flows toward jurisdictions with the planning capacity to apply for and manage grants — which are not always the most vulnerable. Figure 4's upper-right quadrant nations — Vanuatu, Tonga, the Solomon Islands — see little benefit from a strategy oriented around national infrastructure investment, because their binding constraint is fiscal capacity, not infrastructure design. By 2036, the infrastructure-first scenario has measurably improved resilience in high-income urban areas but has not addressed the equity dimension SDG 11 demands. The protection gap between wealthy and vulnerable nations has widened.

*Key uncertainty:* Whether political commitment can be sustained through two or three electoral cycles without a major catastrophic event to sustain public urgency — historically the central failure point of prevention-focused policy.

---

### Scenario 3: Intervention B — Integrated Resilience with Equity Focus

*2026–2036*

The most structurally ambitious scenario couples infrastructure investment with deliberate equity targeting and reformed financial mechanisms. A post-Sendai agreement establishes a global resilience fund — drawing on loss and damage financing mechanisms negotiated at COP — that directs 40% of mitigation investment specifically to high-vulnerability, low-coping-capacity nations identified in the World Risk Index. Domestically, infrastructure investment is conditioned on risk-informed planning requirements: jurisdictions must update their land use regulations before accessing federal grants, attacking the leverage point of new risk creation.

Both the R1 and R2 loops strengthen simultaneously. Infrastructure investment reduces baseline disaster severity; emergency response capacity is expanded in the communities where it matters most (Figure 4's upper-right quadrant); and the reformed planning requirements begin to slow the accumulation of new exposure in high-risk zones. By 2030, SIDS nations show measurable improvements in coping capacity scores — modest but directional.

The B1 constraint remains active but loosens more significantly than in Scenario 2 because the equity-targeted spending reduces the catastrophic tail-risk events that generate the largest recovery cost spikes. Avoided losses in the most vulnerable nations compound over a decade: a country that does not experience a 46%-of-GDP disaster event (Micronesia's 2023 outcome) retains the fiscal capacity to invest in its own resilience in subsequent years. By 2036, the integrated scenario shows improvements on both efficiency (lower loss-per-event) and equity (narrowing protection gap) metrics — though full realization of the investment returns is still 10–15 years away.

*Key uncertainty:* Whether the international political will exists to fund equity-targeted resilience at the scale required, given competing fiscal pressures and the diffuse beneficiaries of prevention spending.

## 3. Leverage Point Analysis

### Identified Leverage Point: Risk-Informed Urban Planning Linked to Financial Incentives

Of all the intervention points available in this system, the highest-leverage opportunity is **mandatory risk-informed urban planning as a condition for accessing disaster financing** — that is, making updated land use regulations, building codes, and hazard exposure assessments a prerequisite for federal mitigation grants, insurance access, and post-disaster recovery funds.

**Why this point is high-leverage:**

Most leverage points in complex systems work by adjusting flows in existing loops. This intervention works differently: it changes the *structure* of the system by interrupting the creation of new risk. Every year that vulnerable development continues in flood plains, coastal zones, and fire-interface areas, the exposure variable in Figure 4 grows — adding future victims, future losses, and future recovery costs to a system already overwhelmed by existing risk. Hardening existing infrastructure addresses accumulated past decisions; risk-informed planning stops the accumulation of new ones.

Donella Meadows identifies "the rules of the system" and "the goals of the system" as among the highest leverage points in any complex system. Conditioning disaster financing on risk-informed planning effectively changes the rules: it makes building in high-risk areas economically costly rather than financially subsidized (as current insurance and recovery frameworks often make it). This operates on the B2 loop most directly — rather than waiting for the Maintenance Reality Check to expose deferred infrastructure costs, this intervention prevents the infrastructure from being built in the wrong place in the first instance.

**Feedback loops affected:**

- **B1 (Budget Constraint):** By reducing new exposure, this intervention reduces future recovery costs — loosening the budget constraint that currently suppresses both infrastructure investment and emergency response funding.
- **B2 (Maintenance Reality Check):** Less vulnerable infrastructure stock means lower maintenance burden and fewer cascading failures during disaster events.
- **R1 (Infrastructure Investment Spiral):** Freed budget from lower recovery costs becomes available for proactive infrastructure investment, allowing the virtuous cycle to strengthen.
- **R2 (Emergency Response Spiral):** Emergency response capacity can be directed toward genuine uncertainty (residual risk) rather than being consumed by predictable losses from poorly sited development.

**Risks and resistance:**

This leverage point faces formidable political and economic resistance. Real estate development interests, municipal governments dependent on property tax revenues, and existing property owners in high-risk areas all have strong incentives to oppose restrictions on where development can occur. The National Flood Insurance Program's history in the United States illustrates this resistance clearly: repeated attempts to reform subsidized flood insurance rates — which currently incentivize building in flood plains — have been scaled back or reversed after political backlash from property owners in vulnerable areas.

There is also an equity risk internal to the intervention: poorly designed planning restrictions can function as exclusionary zoning, preventing affordable housing from being built in lower-risk areas while leaving existing vulnerable populations in place without assistance. Implementation must pair planning restrictions with relocation assistance and affordable housing investment in safer zones — otherwise the intervention solves the physical risk problem while worsening the social equity problem SDG 11 is designed to address.

Finally, there is a temporal resistance: the full benefit of this intervention accrues over 20–30 years as the risky development stock that *wasn't* built gradually reduces the exposure baseline. Political time horizons rarely extend that far, which is precisely why the intervention must be embedded in financial conditions (grant access, insurance pricing) that create immediate economic consequences for non-compliance.

## 4. Implications for the Decision

### What the Analysis Reveals

The systems analysis clarifies that the binary framing of the decision — infrastructure investment *or* emergency response — is itself a product of the Shifting the Burden archetype. It is a false choice generated by a system that has chronically underinvested in prevention and therefore faces a perpetual crisis of emergency response demand. The real question is not which to choose but how to exit the archetype entirely.

The data consistently favors prevention as the higher-return strategy. The 6:1 mitigation return is well-documented; Figure 3 shows the cost of the reactive alternative; and the scenario analysis suggests that infrastructure-first investment begins to bend the loss curve by 2030–2033. Emergency response investment, while necessary and genuinely life-saving, does not address the underlying drivers — it manages consequences of a risk level that continues to rise.

### Which Options Look More Promising

Based on the evidence, **Scenario 3 (Integrated Resilience with Equity Focus)** appears strongest on both efficiency and equity criteria. It addresses the Growth and Underinvestment archetype by sustaining infrastructure investment; it addresses the Shifting the Burden dynamic by building emergency response capacity in the communities where it is most deficient; and it attacks the leverage point of new risk creation through planning reform. Scenario 2 (Infrastructure-First) performs well on efficiency but fails the SDG 11 equity test, leaving the most vulnerable nations behind. Scenario 1 (Status Quo) continues the pattern the data has documented for four decades — accelerating losses, widening inequity, and compounding fiscal constraint.

### Key Uncertainties That Remain

Three uncertainties are material to the Milestone 4 recommendation. First, the political sustainability of prevention investment across electoral cycles — historically the central failure mode. Second, whether international financing mechanisms can be structured to reach the highest-need nations at sufficient scale. Third, whether the leverage point intervention (risk-informed planning) can be designed to avoid becoming an equity-adverse exclusionary tool.

### Preview of Milestone 4 Recommendation

The evidence points toward recommending an integrated investment strategy with a strong prevention emphasis — not a fixed ratio of infrastructure to response, but a structured transition: a 10-year commitment to doubling pre-disaster mitigation investment, conditioned on risk-informed planning adoption, with explicit equity weighting toward high-vulnerability nations and communities. Emergency response capacity should be sustained and targeted toward residual risk rather than grown as a substitute for prevention. Milestone 4 will develop this recommendation in detail, including implementation sequencing, governance mechanisms, and monitoring indicators aligned with the Sendai Framework successor process and SDG 11 targets.
