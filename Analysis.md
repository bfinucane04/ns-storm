# Milestone 3: Analysis — Systems Focus (Path A)

## Resilient by Design: Infrastructure Investment vs. Emergency Response Through the Lens of UN SDG 11

---

## 1. System Archetype Identification

### Primary Archetype: Growth and Underinvestment

The **Growth and Underinvestment** archetype is the dominant structural pattern shaping disaster resilience decisions worldwide, and it maps directly onto the challenges that UN Sustainable Development Goal 11 — "Make cities and human settlements inclusive, safe, resilient and sustainable" — was designed to address.

In the generic Growth and Underinvestment archetype, demand grows toward a performance limit that *could* be raised through investment, but investment is delayed because current performance appears adequate or because the capital required is prohibitive. Performance eventually degrades, the system enters crisis, and the cost of the delayed investment compounds dramatically.

#### Mapping to the Global Disaster Resilience Context

The archetype maps onto the SDG 11 disaster resilience system as follows:

- **Growth (Demand):** The escalating frequency and severity of climate-driven disasters represents the "growth" pressure. Global economic losses from natural catastrophes reached an estimated $417 billion in 2024, a 15% increase above the decade average. Insured losses hit a record $154 billion, surpassing the 10-year average by 27%. For the first time in history, 21 separate incidents in a single year resulted in multi-billion-dollar insurance claims. This is not a gradual trend — it is an accelerating demand curve pressing against infrastructure designed for a climate that no longer exists.

- **Performance Standard (Urban Infrastructure Capacity):** Cities occupy just 3% of Earth's land but account for 60–80% of energy consumption and 75% of carbon emissions. More than half the world's population — over 4 billion people — now lives in urban areas, projected to reach 70% by 2050. The infrastructure serving these populations was largely designed to historical climate norms. SDG Target 11.5 calls for significantly reducing disaster deaths, affected populations, and direct economic losses relative to GDP by 2030, but the infrastructure underpinning these outcomes was built for a world with less extreme weather, less urban concentration, and fewer cascading risks.

- **Underinvestment (Delayed Infrastructure Resilience):** Despite the Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction's Priority 3 — "Investing in disaster risk reduction for resilience" — actual investment remains chronically insufficient. The UNDRR's Global Assessment Report 2025 documents that total disaster costs now exceed $2.3 trillion annually when cascading and ecosystem costs are included. Direct costs averaged $70–80 billion per year between 1970 and 2000 but grew to $180–200 billion between 2001 and 2020. Yet spending on prevention remains a fraction of spending on response: a significant portion of disaster-related funding, particularly in Least Developed Countries, remains focused on response rather than prevention.

- **Performance Gap → Crisis:** When disasters exceed degraded infrastructure's capacity, the results are catastrophic and inequitable. In 2023, North America had the greatest absolute economic exposure at $69.57 billion in direct losses (0.23% of GDP), but Micronesia suffered $4.3 billion in losses representing 46.1% of its subregional GDP. Poorer countries bear a disproportionate economic burden relative to their overall wealth, even though they generate far less absolute damage. The protection gap — uninsured losses — stood at $263 billion in 2024, or 63% of total losses, meaning most of the world's disaster costs fall on governments, households, and communities with no financial cushion.

- **Shifting to Emergency Response (Symptomatic Solution):** Rather than raising the performance limit through preventive infrastructure, many countries rely on post-disaster response and recovery. This dynamic connects to a secondary **Shifting the Burden** archetype: when emergency response performs adequately, it reduces the perceived urgency of infrastructure investment, which is more expensive and politically harder to justify. The cycle persists because response is visible and immediate while prevention succeeds invisibly.

#### Key Variables and Loops Creating the Archetypal Behavior

The following feedback loops from the CLD drive the Growth and Underinvestment pattern:

**R1 — Infrastructure Investment Spiral (Reinforcing):** Higher infrastructure quality reduces disaster severity, which preserves national and municipal budgets, which enables further investment. This virtuous cycle is the mechanism SDG 11.5 implicitly relies upon — build resilient infrastructure, reduce losses, reinvest. But when infrastructure degrades, disaster severity increases, budgets are consumed by recovery, and less money is available for the investment that would break the cycle. The GAR 2025 describes this as a destructive cycle of "disasters, debt, un-insurability and humanitarian need."

**R2 — Emergency Response Capability Spiral (Reinforcing):** As disasters intensify, demand for response grows, which drives funding toward response capacity. The Sendai Framework's Priority 4 — "Enhancing disaster preparedness for effective response" — captures this loop. Greater response capacity reduces immediate suffering, which appears to validate the response approach. This loop competes with R1 for the same limited budgets, creating a structural rivalry between prevention and reaction.

**B1 — Budget Constraint (Balancing):** National and municipal budgets impose hard constraints. As disaster frequency and severity increase, recovery costs consume growing shares of fiscal capacity. The GAR 2025 documents this dynamic directly: when disasters occur, households lose assets and income, shrinking tax revenue; governments borrow more; as debt becomes riskier, interest costs spiral; and eventually there is no budget left for recovery — let alone prevention. Smaller, less resilient economies are hit hardest.

**B2 — Maintenance Reality Check (Balancing):** As infrastructure ages and climate change intensifies, maintenance costs rise. In rapidly urbanizing regions — where 1.2 billion additional people are expected to live in cities by 2050 compared to 2020 — maintenance demands compound as new informal settlements stretch infrastructure beyond designed capacity. Municipalities spend more to maintain degraded systems at their current inadequate level, with nothing left for upgrades.

#### Evidence from Data and Research

The Growth and Underinvestment pattern is extensively documented:

- Global economic losses from natural catastrophes have grown from $70–80 billion annually (1970–2000) to $180–200 billion (2001–2020) to $417 billion in 2024 alone — demonstrating the accelerating demand curve.
- The number of countries with national disaster risk reduction strategies has increased from 57 in 2015 to 131 by October 2024, showing governance progress under the Sendai Framework — but implementation and funding lag far behind policy adoption.
- As of March 2024, only 64% of Small Island Developing States and 60% of Least Developed Countries had national DRR strategies, and these countries face the greatest implementation gaps despite the highest relative vulnerability.
- The World Bank's Disaster Risk Reduction progress score (1–5 scale under the Hyogo Framework) showed significant variation across countries, with many developing nations scoring below 3.0 — indicating that institutional prioritization of DRR remained insufficient even as exposure grew.
- The insurance protection gap ($263 billion uninsured in 2024) demonstrates that market mechanisms alone cannot close the investment deficit, and that government-funded infrastructure resilience remains essential.

### Secondary Archetype: Shifting the Burden

A secondary **Shifting the Burden** archetype reinforces the Growth and Underinvestment pattern. In this structure:

- The **problem symptom** is disaster impact on urban populations — deaths, displacement, infrastructure disruption, economic losses (SDG Indicators 11.5.1, 11.5.2, and 11.5.3).
- The **symptomatic solution** is enhanced emergency response — early warning systems, evacuation capacity, shelter operations, rapid recovery. The Sendai Framework's Priority 4 addresses this directly.
- The **fundamental solution** is resilient infrastructure and risk-informed urban planning that prevents or reduces disaster severity in the first place — the Sendai Framework's Priority 3 and SDG Target 11.b.
- The **side effect** of effective response is reduced urgency for prevention. When early warning systems and emergency coordination save lives during a disaster, the political and institutional pressure to invest in expensive infrastructure upgrades diminishes. The fundamental solution is deferred again.

This dynamic is particularly visible in developing countries where international humanitarian assistance flows primarily after disasters occur, creating a structural incentive to build response capacity (which attracts aid) rather than preventive infrastructure (which does not). The GAR 2025 explicitly identifies this pattern, warning that unless the cycle of costly response and slow recovery is broken, climate-driven disasters may reduce household income growth by 11–29% between now and 2050.

## 2. Scenario Narratives

The following three scenarios trace how the global disaster resilience system might evolve over the next 5–10 years depending on the strategic choices made by national policymakers and the international community. Each scenario references specific feedback loops from the CLD, aligns with SDG 11 indicators, and incorporates quantitative projections grounded in the research evidence.

### Scenario 1: Status Quo — The Sendai Framework Expires Without Structural Change

National policymakers continue current patterns: governance frameworks expand on paper (more countries adopt DRR strategies), but actual investment in infrastructure resilience remains chronically below what is needed. Disaster response absorbs the majority of available funding. The Sendai Framework expires in 2030 without achieving its core targets on loss reduction.

Over the first three years, the system appears to progress. The number of countries reporting national DRR strategies surpasses 140, reaching nearly universal adoption. SDG reporting improves. But the gap between policy adoption and implementation widens. In urbanizing regions of Sub-Saharan Africa, South and Southeast Asia, cities add millions of residents into settlements built without climate-resilient standards. Infrastructure designed for 2010-era climate norms degrades under 2030-era weather extremes.

By years four through six, loop B1 (Budget Constraint) tightens across vulnerable economies. Drawing from GAR 2025 projections, the destructive cycle of disasters, debt, and un-insurability accelerates. Annual disaster costs continue trending above $400 billion globally, but indirect costs — supply chain disruption, lost productivity, health impacts — push the real toll past $2.5 trillion. Small Island Developing States and Least Developed Countries face cascading fiscal crises as disasters consume budgets faster than economies can recover. The insurance protection gap, already at 63% of total losses, widens further in developing regions where coverage remains below 1%.

By years eight through ten, SDG Target 11.5's aspirations — significant reduction in deaths, affected people, and economic losses — are clearly unmet. Disaster mortality trends reverse as urbanization places more people in hazard-exposed areas without corresponding infrastructure upgrades. The post-Sendai successor framework faces the same fundamental problem: countries know what to do but lack the fiscal capacity and political will to invest in prevention at the scale required.

**Key uncertainty:** The pace of climate-driven intensification could accelerate if tipping points are crossed, or moderate if emission reduction commitments (Paris Agreement) deliver faster-than-expected results.

### Scenario 2: Intervention A — Infrastructure-First Investment (Prevention Priority)

The international community, led by multilateral development banks and the successor to the Sendai Framework, redirects disaster financing decisively toward preventive infrastructure. A new global financing mechanism — building on the World Bank's GFDRR model and the Loss and Damage Fund established at COP28 — channels substantial new resources toward climate-resilient infrastructure in the most vulnerable urban areas. National policymakers in participating countries commit to reallocating disaster budgets from a 70/30 response-to-prevention split toward a 40/60 split.

In years one through three, infrastructure projects launch across high-risk urban corridors. Flood control systems, climate-resilient building codes, upgraded stormwater management, and early warning infrastructure receive major capital investment. Loop R1 (Infrastructure Investment Spiral) begins to activate in pilot cities. However, the reallocation reduces emergency response budgets during the construction period, weakening loop R2 (Emergency Response Capability Spiral). Some countries face political backlash as visible response capacity declines while infrastructure projects remain incomplete and invisible.

By years four through six, completed infrastructure begins delivering measurable returns. Countries with upgraded building codes and flood management see 30–50% reductions in direct losses from comparable storm events — consistent with the Hurricane Andrew counterfactual analysis showing that modern building codes reduce residential losses by 50% and commercial losses by 40%. SDG Indicator 11.5.2 (direct economic loss relative to GDP) begins improving in participating countries. Urban areas with hardened infrastructure experience shorter service disruptions (Indicator 11.5.3), and disaster mortality rates (Indicator 11.5.1) decline in protected zones.

By years seven through ten, the compounding returns of prevention investment become visible in national fiscal positions. Countries that invested early have lower debt burdens from disaster recovery, improved credit ratings, and growing fiscal space for continued investment. The GAR 2025's modeling suggests that such investment could push fiscal crises out by 12–19 years for developing economies. However, the reduced response capacity from the early reallocation created real harm: disasters that occurred during the infrastructure build-out period were managed less effectively, and communities outside project areas — particularly informal settlements that are hardest to retrofit — received neither infrastructure protection nor adequate response.

**Key uncertainty:** Infrastructure investments are designed for projected climate conditions, but projections carry significant uncertainty. If actual extremes exceed design standards, the weakened response system faces overwhelming demand in precisely the areas the strategy left exposed.

**Unintended consequence:** Infrastructure investment flows preferentially to areas with formal land tenure, clear property rights, and established governance — systematically excluding the informal settlements where over 1 billion people live in slum-like conditions (SDG Indicator 11.1.1). The most vulnerable populations may see no benefit from a prevention-first strategy.

### Scenario 3: Intervention B — Integrated Resilience (Balanced Portfolio with Equity Focus)

National policymakers adopt an integrated approach that sequences infrastructure investment and response enhancement based on context-specific risk assessment and community vulnerability. This approach aligns with the Sendai Framework's all-of-society model and SDG 11's emphasis on inclusion. Rather than choosing prevention over response, governments target the highest-leverage infrastructure investments first while simultaneously maintaining and modernizing response capacity.

In years one through three, participating countries conduct comprehensive urban risk assessments that map hazard exposure, infrastructure vulnerability, and social vulnerability together. Following the Sendai Framework's Priority 1 (Understanding disaster risk), these assessments reveal that risk is concentrated in specific urban corridors and demographic groups. Initial investment targets three categories simultaneously: critical infrastructure hardening in highest-risk areas (water, power, transport), early warning system modernization (leveraging digital technology to extend coverage to informal settlements), and emergency response capacity in areas where infrastructure upgrades are not yet feasible.

By years four through six, early infrastructure projects deliver returns that partially fund the next phase — activating a moderated version of loop R1. Modernized early warning systems, now reaching populations previously excluded, reduce disaster mortality (Indicator 11.5.1) even in areas where infrastructure has not yet been upgraded. The integrated data from risk assessments improves both infrastructure maintenance prioritization (addressing loop B2 more efficiently) and response coordination. Countries use the Sendai Framework Monitor's 38 indicators and the SDG reporting infrastructure to track progress and direct resources to the highest-impact interventions.

By years seven through ten, the portfolio approach produces differentiated resilience across urban areas — some zones have hardened infrastructure, others rely on enhanced early warning and response, and the most vulnerable communities have received targeted interventions addressing their specific risk profiles. The approach preserves international humanitarian cooperation (countries maintain response capacity for domestic and cross-border mutual aid) while building the infrastructure base that reduces future demand for humanitarian assistance. The equity focus ensures that SDG 11's emphasis on protecting "the poor and people in vulnerable situations" (Target 11.5) translates into measurable outcomes for populations in informal settlements, not just formal urban areas.

**Key uncertainty:** The portfolio approach requires sustained political commitment across multiple government cycles and coordinated action between national, municipal, and international actors. The complexity may exceed governance capacity in countries with weaker institutional frameworks — precisely those most vulnerable to disasters. Additionally, the balanced approach means neither infrastructure nor response reaches the investment levels a single-focus strategy would achieve, risking adequacy in both domains but excellence in neither.

**Unintended consequence:** The data infrastructure required for risk-informed prioritization could become a gatekeeping mechanism — countries that cannot produce high-quality risk assessments may be excluded from international financing streams, deepening the gap between data-rich and data-poor nations.

## 3. Leverage Point Analysis

### Most Promising Leverage Point: Risk-Informed Urban Planning Linked to Financial Incentives

The single most promising leverage point in this system is the integration of **disaster risk data into urban planning and development finance decisions** — not because it is the largest possible investment, but because it simultaneously affects multiple feedback loops and connects the SDG 11 governance framework to actual resource allocation.

#### Why This Point Offers High Impact Relative to Effort

Urban planning sits at the intersection of all the system's major dynamics because it determines where people live, what infrastructure serves them, and how exposed they are to hazards before any disaster occurs. SDG Target 11.b calls for cities to adopt "integrated policies and plans towards inclusion, resource efficiency, mitigation and adaptation to climate change, resilience to disasters." As of October 2024, 131 countries report having national DRR strategies — the governance architecture exists. The leverage point is converting that governance into binding urban planning requirements that redirect development finance.

This intervention is high-leverage because:

- **Prevention function:** Risk-informed planning prevents new risk creation by directing urban growth away from high-hazard areas and requiring climate-resilient building standards for new construction. This is far cheaper than retrofitting existing infrastructure — you avoid building the problem rather than fixing it afterward. By 2050, 1.2 billion additional people will live in cities; where and how those cities expand determines whether future disaster costs compound or stabilize.

- **Response function:** Risk data embedded in urban planning improves emergency response by pre-identifying evacuation routes, shelter locations, and critical infrastructure dependencies. Planners who understand hazard exposure can design redundancy into systems (alternative water sources, distributed power generation, multiple transport corridors) that makes response inherently more effective when events do occur.

- **Financial function:** When risk data is linked to development finance — requiring climate risk assessments for infrastructure loans, conditioning municipal bonds on resilience standards, adjusting insurance pricing to reflect actual risk — it creates market incentives that amplify government investment. The insurance industry's growing unwillingness to cover high-risk areas without mitigation measures creates natural pressure toward prevention.

#### What Feedback Loops It Affects

This leverage point intervenes in all four of the system's key feedback loops:

1. **R1 (Infrastructure Investment Spiral):** Risk-informed planning ensures that new infrastructure is built to resilient standards from the start, activating R1's virtuous cycle without requiring expensive retrofits. Every new building, road, and utility system built to climate-resilient standards incrementally raises the performance ceiling.

2. **R2 (Emergency Response Capability Spiral):** Better risk data improves response targeting and efficiency. Emergency coordinators who know which communities face which hazards can pre-position resources, design effective warning systems, and coordinate evacuations more effectively — enhancing response capacity without proportional budget increases.

3. **B1 (Budget Constraint):** Preventing new risk creation is orders of magnitude cheaper than retrofitting existing infrastructure or rebuilding after disasters. Risk-informed planning eases the budget constraint by reducing the future cost burden, creating fiscal space for both infrastructure investment and response capacity.

4. **B2 (Maintenance Reality Check):** Infrastructure built to resilient standards requires less maintenance and is less likely to fail during extreme events, reducing the maintenance treadmill that currently consumes budgets in rapidly urbanizing areas.

#### Risks and Resistance

Several risks could undermine this leverage point:

- **Governance capacity:** Effective risk-informed planning requires technical expertise in hazard modeling, urban design, and financial analysis that many municipalities and national agencies lack — particularly in the Least Developed Countries and Small Island Developing States where vulnerability is highest. Without capacity building, the leverage point becomes another unfunded mandate.

- **Informal settlements:** Over 1 billion people live in informal settlements outside formal planning systems. Risk-informed urban planning may improve outcomes for future formal development while leaving existing informal communities unprotected. Planning frameworks must explicitly address informality to avoid deepening inequity.

- **Political resistance:** Risk data may reveal that politically connected development is located in high-hazard areas, creating pressure to suppress or ignore risk information. Developers, landowners, and municipal officials may resist planning restrictions that reduce property values or block construction projects.

- **Data gaps:** The UNDRR notes that 96% of disaster reconstruction cost data and 42% of total estimated damage data is missing from global databases. Risk-informed planning requires risk data that many countries cannot yet produce. Closing these gaps requires sustained investment in monitoring, modeling, and data infrastructure.

Despite these risks, risk-informed urban planning linked to financial incentives represents the highest-leverage intervention because it operates on the *creation* of new risk rather than the management of existing risk — addressing the root cause of the Growth and Underinvestment archetype rather than its symptoms.

## 4. Implications for the Decision

This analysis reveals that the choice between storm infrastructure investment and emergency response capacity is fundamentally shaped by a Growth and Underinvestment archetype operating at global scale. Decades of insufficient investment in climate-resilient infrastructure — despite the frameworks, targets, and governance architectures created by SDG 11 and the Sendai Framework — have produced a widening gap between climate-driven disaster demand and urban system capacity. The secondary Shifting the Burden dynamic means that each effective emergency response operation reduces the perceived urgency of the prevention investment needed to avoid future crises, perpetuating the underinvestment cycle.

The scenario analysis suggests that neither a pure prevention-first nor a pure response-first strategy is optimal when evaluated against SDG 11's integrated vision of "inclusive, safe, resilient and sustainable" cities. The infrastructure-first approach (Scenario 2) delivers the strongest long-term economic returns — the GAR 2025's modeling shows it can defer fiscal crises by 12–19 years in vulnerable economies — but creates dangerous short-term gaps in response capacity and systematically excludes informal settlements from protection. The status quo (Scenario 1) leads toward compounding failure as the Sendai Framework expires in 2030 without meeting its loss-reduction targets. The integrated portfolio approach (Scenario 3) offers the most robust and equitable path, but demands governance sophistication and sustained political commitment that many of the most vulnerable countries struggle to maintain.

The leverage point analysis identifies risk-informed urban planning linked to financial incentives as the most promising entry point because it operates on the creation of new risk — a far cheaper intervention than retrofitting or rebuilding — and connects existing governance frameworks (the 131 countries with DRR strategies) to actual resource allocation decisions. With 1.2 billion additional urban residents expected by 2050, the decisions made now about where and how cities expand will determine whether future disaster costs compound or stabilize.

Key uncertainties remain: the pace and pattern of climate intensification, the durability of international financing commitments, the governance capacity of the most vulnerable nations, and the persistent data gaps that undermine risk-informed decision-making. The recommendation to be developed in Milestone 4 will likely advocate for a phased strategy that begins with embedding risk data into urban planning and development finance as the highest-leverage entry point, followed by targeted infrastructure hardening in the highest-risk urban corridors, while maintaining emergency response capacity sufficient to manage the failures that no infrastructure investment can fully prevent — with explicit equity provisions ensuring that informal settlements and vulnerable populations are not left behind by a system designed primarily for formal urban areas.
