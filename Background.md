# Background: Storm Infrastructure Investment vs. Enhanced Emergency Response Capacity

## Why This Decision Matters

Municipal emergency coordinators face an increasingly urgent strategic choice as extreme weather events intensify: should they invest limited budgets in upgrading storm infrastructure to prevent disasters, or enhance emergency response capacity to better manage disasters when they occur? This decision carries profound implications for public safety, economic resilience, fiscal responsibility, and community well-being in an era where infrastructure built to climatological norms of 20-40 years ago no longer adequately withstands current weather extremes.

The stakes are extraordinary and accelerating. Since 1980, severe storms have caused over $383 billion in total damages in the United States, with climate change-related events estimated to have cost more than $1.0 trillion (in 2021 dollars) over this period. The frequency and severity of extreme weather events have escalated dramatically—before the 2010s, a billion-dollar storm could be expected every few years, but 29 of 37 billion-dollar storms since Hurricane Katrina occurred in the last decade alone. According to Climate Central, over the last five years there were 16 days on average between U.S. billion-dollar disasters, compared with 82 days in the 1980s. In 2023, that number shrank to just 12 days between disasters, leaving less time and fewer resources for federal, state, and local governments to respond, recover quickly, and prepare for future risks.

The economic case for prevention is compelling but complex. Research demonstrates that $1 in pre-disaster hazard mitigation and preparedness spending avoids at least $6 in disaster response and rebuilding costs. A study conducted for the Insurance Institute for Business & Home Safety found that losses from Hurricane Andrew, which caused more than $20 billion in insured damage, would have been reduced by 50 percent for residential properties and 40 percent for commercial properties if buildings were constructed according to Florida's 2004 statewide building code. Florida Power & Light's decade-long investment in both infrastructure hardening and smart grid automation enabled the utility to restore power to millions of customers quickly after Hurricane Ian in 2022, demonstrating that integrated strategies combining durable infrastructure with intelligent operations deliver measurable returns.

However, the infrastructure investment required is staggering. Infrastructure was built to climatological norms that no longer reflect current reality, accounting for typical severe weather events experienced regionally but not planning for withstanding extreme weather events that are now becoming routine. As extreme weather events become more common, critical infrastructure sites need to revisit building codes, material limitations, and regional damages during past severe events to address areas at greatest immediate risk. Much of U.S. distribution system infrastructure was built decades ago and is now reaching the end of its intended service life, creating higher risks of breakdowns, rising maintenance costs, and longer recovery times even before extreme weather strikes.

## What Makes This Decision Difficult

The infrastructure-versus-response dilemma creates profound tension between prevention and reaction, between long-term investment and immediate capability, and between protecting property and protecting people. These tensions are not merely technical—they reflect fundamentally different philosophies about risk management, government responsibility, and community resilience.

The temporal paradox lies at the heart of the difficulty. Infrastructure upgrades require massive upfront capital investment with benefits that materialize over decades, while emergency response capabilities provide immediate, visible capacity to save lives and restore normalcy after disasters. In 2022, U.S. customers experienced an average of 5.6 hours without power across 1.4 service interruptions per year—the longest average outage duration in more than a decade, underscoring that interruptions are becoming both more frequent and more prolonged. Emergency coordinators must choose between preventing these outages through infrastructure investment or managing them more effectively through enhanced response when they inevitably occur.

The cost-effectiveness calculation is frustratingly uncertain. While the 6:1 benefit-cost ratio for mitigation appears decisive, this ratio depends heavily on assumptions about future storm frequency, severity, and location—all of which are changing in unpredictable ways as climate patterns shift. Traditional cost-benefit analysis primarily measures physical risk to property while neglecting the long-term, intangible social costs incurred by vulnerable communities. FEMA's methodology for calculating costs and benefits captures direct loss of property and life but is less successful at estimating the indirect burdens that natural disasters impose on vulnerable communities and government. Under FEMA's current system, areas with lower property values are less likely to qualify for infrastructure projects that are similarly priced to those built in high-property-value areas, creating an equity challenge that complicates purely economic analysis.

The capacity constraint creates an additional dilemma. Most municipalities lack sufficient resources to fully invest in both infrastructure upgrades and emergency response enhancement. Budget limitations force genuine trade-offs. Money spent undergrounding power lines or upgrading stormwater systems cannot be spent on emergency vehicles, training programs, or mutual aid agreements. The old model of reacting to damage after the fact is no longer sufficient, yet the new model of comprehensive infrastructure resilience remains prohibitively expensive for most municipalities.

The prediction problem compounds the difficulty. Infrastructure investments must be designed for specific hazards—flood control systems differ from wind-resistant designs differ from extreme heat mitigation. But climate change is introducing nonstationary stressors where the frequency and magnitude of weather patterns no longer remain unchanged into the future. Municipalities may invest heavily in flood protection only to face unprecedented wildfires, or vice versa. Emergency response capabilities, by contrast, provide flexibility to address whatever disaster actually occurs, though potentially less effectively than targeted prevention would have.

The political economy presents its own challenges. Infrastructure projects create visible construction activity and ribbon-cutting opportunities but lack the dramatic narrative of emergency responders saving lives during disasters. Response capabilities generate compelling stories and grateful constituents, while preventive infrastructure succeeds by ensuring nothing dramatic happens—an outcome difficult to celebrate or attribute directly to specific investments. This creates perverse incentives favoring reactive response over proactive prevention.

## Key Stakeholders

This decision affects multiple stakeholders with competing priorities, varying levels of vulnerability, and different time horizons for evaluating success:

**Municipal Residents** are the ultimate beneficiaries or victims of infrastructure and response decisions. Their interests are not monolithic—homeowners in flood-prone areas desperately want upgraded stormwater infrastructure, while residents in areas with low flood risk may prefer investments elsewhere. Low-income residents disproportionately suffer from infrastructure failures, lacking resources to evacuate quickly, repair damage, or relocate after disasters. They rely heavily on effective emergency response when disasters strike. Meanwhile, wealthier residents may have private generators, flood insurance, and resources to weather disruptions, making them less dependent on either infrastructure protection or emergency response.

**The Emergency Coordinator** bears professional and ethical responsibility for protecting public safety with limited resources. The coordinator faces accountability pressures that favor visible response capability over invisible prevention. When disasters strike, the coordinator's performance is immediately scrutinized—did emergency services respond quickly? Were evacuation orders timely? Were shelters adequate? By contrast, infrastructure failures are diffuse and attributable to multiple actors. This creates professional incentives favoring response capabilities even when prevention might be more cost-effective.

**Utility Companies** own and operate much of the critical infrastructure vulnerable to extreme weather—power grids, water systems, telecommunications networks. More than 90% of power interruptions occur on the distribution system where infrastructure was built decades ago. Utilities make their own infrastructure investment decisions but depend on municipal support for permitting, right-of-way access, and coordination during emergencies. Enhanced municipal emergency response capabilities can help utilities restore service faster after outages, but utilities ultimately prefer infrastructure investments that prevent outages entirely.

**Municipal Budget Officials** must balance competing demands across all city services—not just emergency management but also schools, public works, parks, and police. Infrastructure investments require large capital expenditures, often through bond financing with multi-decade repayment obligations. Emergency response capabilities require ongoing operational funding for personnel, training, equipment, and facilities. Budget officials prefer investments with measurable returns and may be skeptical of prevention spending that cannot demonstrate counterfactual benefits—how do you prove a disaster was prevented?

**Federal and State Emergency Management Agencies** provide funding, coordination, and support during disasters. FEMA's Building Resilient Infrastructure and Communities (BRIC) program prioritizes pre-disaster mitigation and offers a 6 percent set-aside from the Disaster Relief Fund specifically for preparedness. However, historically, post-disaster assistance has received significantly more funding than pre-disaster mitigation. FEMA also provides post-disaster funding through Public Assistance (infrastructure repair), Individual Assistance (household support), and Hazard Mitigation Grant Program (reducing future losses). The agency's cost-benefit requirements create barriers for some mitigation projects while making post-disaster recovery funds relatively easy to access.

**Insurance Companies and Property Owners** face direct financial consequences from infrastructure failures. Extreme weather events are increasing the number of businesses and homes considered uninsurable in the private market, leaving government—and therefore American taxpayers—liable for costs and risks as the "insurer of last recourse." According to the Insurance Information Institute, total hurricane-related risk insured by government has increased 15-fold since 1990 to $885 billion. Insurance companies prefer infrastructure investments that reduce claims frequency and severity. Some insurers offer premium discounts for communities with strong building codes and infrastructure, creating market incentives for prevention.

**Neighboring Municipalities and Regional Partners** coordinate mutual aid agreements for emergency response. When disasters overwhelm local capacity, neighboring jurisdictions send fire crews, medical teams, search-and-rescue units, and other resources. These mutual aid relationships depend on all participating municipalities maintaining adequate response capabilities. If one municipality shifts resources entirely toward infrastructure and away from response, it may become a net recipient rather than contributor to regional mutual aid systems, straining regional resilience.

**Construction and Engineering Industries** benefit economically from infrastructure investment decisions. These stakeholders advocate for preventive infrastructure projects, employing local workers and generating economic activity. However, their financial interest creates potential conflicts—they profit whether infrastructure actually reduces disaster impacts or not. Similarly, emergency response equipment vendors and training organizations benefit from response capability investments, creating their own potential conflicts of interest in advising municipalities.

## What Has Been Tried Before

For much of the 20th century, emergency management in the United States followed a reactive model. Federal, state, and local governments built response capabilities—fire departments, ambulance services, emergency operations centers—but invested relatively little in disaster prevention. Infrastructure was built to historical weather norms with the assumption of "climate stationarity," where frequency and magnitude of weather patterns remain unchanged into the future. This approach worked reasonably well when extreme weather events were truly extreme—rare enough that building to prevent them seemed economically unjustifiable.

The reactive model began showing serious cracks with Hurricane Andrew in 1992, which caused $27 billion in damage (1992 dollars) and exposed fundamental weaknesses in both infrastructure and response systems. Andrew prompted Florida to adopt significantly stricter building codes, demonstrating that prevention through regulation could substantially reduce disaster costs. Studies showed that if buildings had been constructed to Florida's later 2004 statewide building code, Andrew's losses would have been reduced by 50 percent for residential properties and 40 percent for commercial properties.

Hurricane Katrina in 2005 represented a watershed moment, causing catastrophic failures across infrastructure, emergency response, and coordination systems. The disaster exposed how infrastructure built to inadequate standards (levees designed for Category 3 hurricanes when a Category 5 struck) combined with ineffective emergency response created compounding failures. President George W. Bush signed the Post-Katrina Emergency Management Reform Act of 2006 to overhaul FEMA, creating regional offices and stronger protections for vulnerable populations. The Obama administration later expanded flexibility for states and Tribes in using recovery funds and issued the Preparing the United States for the Impacts of Climate Change Executive Order directing federal agencies to integrate climate preparedness into operations.

The Disaster Recovery Reform Act of 2018 marked a significant shift toward prevention by creating the Building Resilient Infrastructure and Communities (BRIC) program, which set aside 6 percent of FEMA's Disaster Relief Fund specifically for pre-disaster mitigation and resilience projects. BRIC represents recognition that prevention is more cost-effective than reaction. The program prioritizes innovative, equitable, and nature-based infrastructure projects, and as part of Justice40, ensures a minimum of 40 percent of benefits target historically disadvantaged communities. The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act of 2021 doubled BRIC funding levels, providing $1.5 billion over two years for community resilience projects before disasters occur.

Some municipalities and utilities have pioneered integrated approaches combining infrastructure and response. Florida Power & Light invested over a decade in both physical infrastructure (undergrounding lines in high-risk areas, upgrading poles and equipment) and smart grid technology (thousands of sensors and automated switches, digital twin simulations). During Hurricane Ian in 2022, FPL's integrated approach enabled rapid, selective restoration of power to millions of customers. The utility wasn't just building stronger infrastructure—it had built a more intelligent system capable of both preventing some failures and recovering quickly from others.

Miami has invested extensively in flood prevention through pump stations, raised roadways, and revised building codes to combat increasing problems from rising sea levels and storm surges. The city exemplifies targeted infrastructure investment addressing known, specific vulnerabilities. Conversely, Asheville, North Carolina demonstrated the value of specific preventive measures when an auxiliary spillway completed in 2021 prevented dam failure during Hurricane Helene despite overwhelming rainfall, even as other infrastructure failed.

Louisiana consolidated coastal restoration and hurricane protection responsibilities under a single government authority in 2005 following Hurricanes Katrina and Rita. The Coastal Protection and Restoration Authority now oversees nearly $2 billion in projects designed to improve coastal resilience, including nature-based solutions that provide multiple benefits. Florida established the Resilient Florida Grants Program in 2022 to fund projects that enhance natural systems to help mitigate flood impacts, improve water quality, and prevent coastal erosion.

Despite these advances, significant challenges remain. Rural communities often lack capacity to access competitive federal grants—they don't have the people, financial resources, expertise, or time to compete successfully for BRIC and similar programs. Most rural places lack resilience because they have limited capacity to prepare for predictable natural disasters or respond effectively afterward. Isolation creates barriers as emergency services must travel longer distances, and infrastructure in rural areas is often in relatively poor condition with fewer assets and resources to deploy.

Current approaches also face structural limitations. FEMA's cost-benefit analysis requirements create barriers for many communities, particularly those with lower property values. Less than 1 percent of BRIC projects from fiscal years 2020-2023 primarily addressed extreme heat, despite it being the leading weather-related cause of death in the United States. There has never been a presidentially declared major disaster for an extreme heat event, meaning affected communities cannot access FEMA's post-disaster assistance programs. This reveals how current frameworks may not adequately address evolving climate threats.

Recent political disruption adds uncertainty. In April 2025, FEMA suspended both the BRIC and Hazard Mitigation Assistance programs, which funded flood control, wildfire mitigation, and infrastructure resilience projects. The agency also reduced staff and training capacity by 20 percent, limiting its ability to coordinate disaster response and recovery. The Trump administration announced cancellation of grants worth $1.7 billion aimed at preparing communities for extreme weather events and improving air and water quality. These policy shifts represent a return toward reactive response models and away from preventive infrastructure investment, creating confusion for municipal planners attempting to develop long-term resilience strategies.

## Datasets to Be Used

1. **FEMA Disaster Declarations Database (OpenFEMA)**
   - Comprehensive records of all federally declared disasters since 1953
   - https://www.fema.gov/about/openfema/data-sets
   - Includes disaster type, location, declaration date, funding allocated

2. **NOAA Billion-Dollar Weather and Climate Disasters Database**
   - Tracks U.S. weather and climate events with losses exceeding $1 billion (inflation-adjusted)
   - https://www.ncei.noaa.gov/access/billions/
   - Provides cost estimates, affected regions, event types since 1980

3. **FEMA National Risk Index**
   - County-level data on natural hazard risk, vulnerability, and resilience
   - https://hazards.fema.gov/nri/
   - Published November 2021, updated periodically
   - Includes 18 natural hazards with expected annual losses

4. **National Transit Database (NTD) - Infrastructure Condition Data**
   - State of good repair metrics for public infrastructure
   - https://www.transit.dot.gov/ntd
   - Asset age, condition ratings, maintenance costs

5. **FEMA Building Resilient Infrastructure and Communities (BRIC) Grant Data**
   - Project-level data on pre-disaster mitigation funding
   - Available through OpenFEMA
   - Includes project type, location, funding amount, benefit-cost ratios

6. **U.S. Census Bureau - American Community Survey (ACS)**
   - Socioeconomic data for vulnerability assessment
   - https://www.census.gov/programs-surveys/acs
   - Income, housing characteristics, demographics by geography

7. **NOAA Storm Events Database**
   - Detailed records of storm events, locations, damages, fatalities
   - https://www.ncdc.noaa.gov/stormevents/
   - Includes event narratives, damage estimates, affected counties

8. **Municipal Budget and Financial Data**
   - Operating budgets for emergency services, capital budgets for infrastructure
   - Available from municipal finance departments or state repositories
   - Enables cost analysis and budget trade-off modeling

9. **Utility Outage Data**
   - Power outage frequency, duration, affected customers
   - Available from state public utility commissions and utilities
   - Demonstrates infrastructure resilience performance

10. **CDC WONDER - Weather-Related Mortality Data**
    - Deaths attributed to extreme weather events
    - https://wonder.cdc.gov/
    - Provides public health impact metrics for cost-benefit analysis
