#Resilient by Design: Storm Infrastructure vs. Emergency Response

#Decision Statement
Should a municipal emergency coordinator invest limited budget resources in upgraded storm infrastructure to prevent disasters or enhanced emergency response capacity to better manage disasters when they occur, given the increasing frequency and intensity of extreme weather events?

#Executive Summary
Municipal emergency coordinators across the United States face an increasingly critical strategic dilemma that will define their communities' resilience for decades: allocate scarce resources toward preventing disasters through upgraded infrastructure, or toward managing disasters through enhanced emergency response capabilities. This decision carries profound implications for public safety, economic stability, and community survival as extreme weather events transform from exceptional occurrences into routine threats.

The financial stakes are staggering and accelerating. Since 1980, severe storms have caused over $383 billion in total damages across the United States, with climate change-related events estimated to have cost more than $1.0 trillion over this period. The pace has dramatically intensified—before the 2010s, billion-dollar storms occurred every few years, but 29 of 37 such storms since Hurricane Katrina have occurred in the last decade alone. Most alarmingly, the time between billion-dollar disasters has collapsed from 82 days on average in the 1980s to just 16 days in recent years, and in 2023 that interval shrank to merely 12 days. This compression leaves municipalities with less time and fewer resources to recover between events, forcing emergency coordinators to make impossible choices about where to invest their limited budgets.
The economic case for prevention appears compelling: research consistently demonstrates that $1 invested in pre-disaster hazard mitigation and preparedness avoids at least $6 in disaster response and rebuilding costs. A study of Hurricane Andrew—which caused more than $20 billion in insured damage—found that losses would have been reduced by 50 percent for residential properties and 40 percent for commercial properties if buildings had been constructed to Florida's modern building codes. Florida Power & Light's decade-long investment in both infrastructure hardening and smart grid automation proved its value during Hurricane Ian in 2022, enabling rapid restoration of power to millions of customers. These examples suggest that infrastructure investment delivers measurable, quantifiable returns.
However, the case for enhanced emergency response is equally compelling and addresses different but equally critical vulnerabilities. Infrastructure will inevitably fail—no system can be hardened against all possible extreme weather scenarios, especially as climate change introduces unprecedented "nonstationary" conditions where historical weather patterns no longer predict future risks. When infrastructure does fail, effective emergency response saves lives, reduces suffering, and accelerates recovery. In 2022, U.S. customers experienced an average of 5.6 hours without power across 1.4 service interruptions per year—the longest outage duration in more than a decade. Enhanced response capabilities help communities weather these failures through rapid evacuations, efficient shelter operations, coordinated rescue efforts, and accelerated service restoration.

The decision is particularly difficult because it involves genuine trade-offs between different types of value and different time horizons. Infrastructure investments require massive upfront capital expenditures with benefits that materialize over decades, while emergency response capabilities provide immediate capacity to save lives and restore normalcy after disasters. Much of existing U.S. infrastructure was built decades ago to climatological norms that assumed weather patterns would remain stable—an assumption climate change has invalidated. This aging infrastructure is reaching the end of its intended service life, creating failures even in normal conditions and catastrophic collapses during extreme weather. Yet replacing this infrastructure comprehensively would cost trillions of dollars that municipalities simply do not have.
The challenge is further complicated by structural inequities in how disasters affect communities. FEMA's cost-benefit analysis methodology, which determines eligibility for federal mitigation funding, primarily measures physical risk to property while neglecting long-term social costs to vulnerable communities. This approach results in higher-property-value communities receiving disproportionate shares of infrastructure protection, while lower-income communities either remain unprotected or are relocated. Emergency response capabilities, by contrast, serve all residents regardless of property value, providing a more equitable—if reactive—form of protection. This creates a tension between economic efficiency (invest where property values are highest) and social equity (protect those most vulnerable to harm).

Recent political developments add uncertainty to an already complex decision. In April 2025, FEMA suspended its Building Resilient Infrastructure and Communities (BRIC) program and Hazard Mitigation Assistance grants, which had provided billions for pre-disaster mitigation projects. The agency also reduced staff and training capacity by 20 percent, limiting coordination capabilities precisely when extreme weather events are intensifying. The cancellation of $1.7 billion in climate preparedness grants represents a policy shift back toward reactive response models and away from preventive infrastructure investment, leaving municipal emergency coordinators uncertain about federal support for either approach.
